Part 18 — Troubleshooting Embedded Linux

Stay calm, work a checklist, read the error.

TL;DR

Most problems are common and have common causes. Don't panic — work through them one at a time.

Nothing on the console screen? The usual culprit is the wrong speed setting, or a loose wire.

Device won't start or keeps restarting? Think power first. And remember: if a change broke it, the answer is often "restore your backup".

Nothing appears on the console

When you watch the device start over UART (Part 3), you expect text to scroll by. If the screen stays blank, check these in order:

  1. Wiring. Are the connections firm and in the right places? A wire that's fallen out shows exactly this — nothing.
  2. Ground. The adapter and the device must share a common ground connection. Without it, you often get silence.
  3. Speed (baud). Serial connections only work when both sides agree on a speed, measured in baud. If your program is set to the wrong speed, you see nothing or garbage.

Garbled text — random symbols instead of readable words — almost always means the speed is wrong. Try a different common speed until the text turns readable. That's a normal part of finding the right setting; it harms nothing.

The device won't start, or keeps restarting

A device that powers on, gets partway, then starts over again and again is in a boot loop. It's stuck repeating the beginning of startup. Common causes:

  • Power trouble (see below) — the most frequent cause by far.
  • A recent change to the device's software that left it unable to finish starting.
  • A hardware fault, which is less common but possible.

If a boot loop began right after you changed something, treat your change as the prime suspect.

Power problems

Weak or wrong power causes strange, misleading faults — random restarts, freezes, or nothing at all. Check the supply is the correct one for the device, that the cable and connector are sound, and that you're not powering it from something too weak. Power is cheap to rule out, so rule it out first.

Reading an error instead of fearing it

An error message is not an insult — it's a clue, often the best one you have. When you see one:

  • Read it slowly, word by word. Ordinary words in it usually mean what they say.
  • Note the last thing that worked before the error. The trouble is usually right after it.
  • Copy the exact wording and search for it. Someone has almost always seen it before.

When the answer is "restore your backup"

If the device stopped starting right after a change you made to its storage, the calm fix is to write your backup (from Part 15) back over the storage. That returns the device to the known-good state you saved. If even that won't run, the bootloader itself often offers a recovery path — Part 10 covered reaching and using it.

This is exactly why the course insisted on backups before any change. A backup turns a scary "it won't start" into a routine "restore and try again".

Try it yourself (safe)

For each symptom, decide the most likely cause. Answers are hidden below — think first.

  1. You connected UART and see nothing at all on screen.
  2. You see steady streams of random symbols.
  3. The device reboots every couple of seconds and never finishes.

This is a thinking exercise only. Don't power a mains device with its case open, and unplug it before touching any wiring.

Show the most likely causes
  1. Blank screen: check wiring, a shared ground, then the speed setting.
  2. Garbled symbols: wrong speed (baud) — try another common speed.
  3. Constant reboots: power first, then any recent change you made.

Recap

  • Work a checklist calmly — most faults are common and known.
  • Blank console: wiring, ground, speed. Garbled text: wrong speed.
  • Constant restarts (a boot loop): suspect power first.
  • An error message is a clue — read it, note the last thing that worked, search it.
  • Broke it with a change? Restore your backup (Part 15); recover via the bootloader (Part 10) if needed.
