Part 2 — UART: The Hidden Console

The easiest window into an embedded device.

TL;DR

A console is a text window that shows what a device is doing as it starts and runs — and often lets you type commands back to it.

UART is a simple wired link, over just a few wires, that carries that text between the device and your computer.

It uses three wires: one the device talks on, one it listens on, and one shared reference. It is usually the easiest way in.

The single rule that keeps you safe: match the voltage, and never connect blindly. We do not wire anything up yet.

What is a "console"?

When Linux runs on a normal PC, it can print messages to the screen and read what you type on the keyboard. That two-way text channel is called a console.

The Nokia G-010G-P has no screen and no keyboard socket. But the software inside it still wants to print messages and still can accept typed commands — it just needs somewhere to send them. UART is that "somewhere."

With a console you can watch the device wake up line by line: the starter program, then the operating system loading, then the networking software coming online. On many devices you can also press a key during start-up and type commands directly.

What is UART, in plain terms?

UART stands for Universal Asynchronous Receiver / Transmitter. Ignore the long name. In practice UART is just a tiny, old, extremely common way for two devices to send text to each other over a couple of wires, one character at a time.

Think of it like two people passing notes through a slot. One slot is for sending, one slot is for receiving, and they both agree on how fast to pass notes so nothing gets scrambled. That agreed speed is called the baud rate — we will meet it properly later.

Why is it the easiest window in?

Because it is built into the device on purpose. Engineers use UART to develop and debug the product, so the "door" is usually already there on the board — you just have to find it. No network login, no password screen; the messages simply pour out as the device runs.

The three wires

A basic UART link needs only three connections. The names describe them from the device's point of view:

WireShort nameWhat it does
TransmitTXThe device talks — sends its messages out.
ReceiveRXThe device listens — takes in commands you type.
GroundGNDThe shared reference both sides measure against.

The important trick: one side's "talk" wire must reach the other side's "listen" wire. So the device's TX goes to your adapter's RX, and the device's RX goes to your adapter's TX. They cross over. GND simply connects straight across to GND.

What gear do you need?

  • A USB-to-UART adapter — a small board that plugs into your computer's USB port and turns UART's wire signals into something the computer reads as a serial port.
  • A few jumper wires — thin wires with plug or socket ends to join the adapter to the board.
  • A terminal program on your computer — software that opens the serial port and shows the text (and lets you type). Nothing exotic; free ones exist for every system.

The big rule: match the voltage. Different boards send their UART signals at different voltage levels. Connecting a higher-voltage adapter to a lower-voltage device can permanently damage it. You must find out your device's UART voltage and set your adapter to the same level before connecting anything. We cover how to check that on your own unit in Parts 12–13 — do not guess it, and do not connect blindly.

Where the UART pins actually are on the Nokia G-010G-P, and what voltage they use, is something you will read and verify on your own device later. This part is about understanding the idea, not touching the board.

Examples

Watching a start-up. You open the terminal program, plug in the adapter, power the device, and text scrolls past: the starter program reporting in, then the operating system loading, then networking coming up. It is like reading the device's diary out loud, live.

Catching an error. A device that fails to come online often prints why on the console — a missing file, a failed check — even when its lights just blink unhelpfully.

The crossover in real life. If you wire TX to TX by mistake, you get silence: both sides are talking into each other's mouths and nobody is listening. Swapping to the crossover fixes it.

Try it yourself (safe)

No wiring yet — this exercise is pure research.

  1. Search online for "USB-to-UART adapter" and look at a few photos. Notice the USB end and the little row of labelled pins (you will often see TX, RX and GND printed right on the board).
  2. Read one short article or watch one short video of someone reading a device's boot messages over UART. Just observe what the scrolling text looks like.
  3. Find where an adapter's voltage is set (many have a small jumper or switch). Note that this is the setting you would match to a device.

Do not connect anything to the Nokia G-010G-P in this part. We have not found its pins or confirmed its voltage yet. Connecting blindly is exactly the mistake that damages boards.

Self-check: why do TX and RX have to cross over?

Because "talk" only means something if it reaches the other side's "listen." If both sides send on the same wire, neither is receiving — you get silence. Crossing device-talk to adapter-listen (and back) lets each side hear the other.

Recap

  • A console is a live text window into a device as it starts and runs.
  • UART is a simple few-wire link that carries that text to your computer.
  • Three wires: TX (device talks), RX (device listens), GND (shared reference).
  • TX and RX cross over; GND connects straight across.
  • You need a USB-to-UART adapter, jumper wires and a terminal program.
  • Always match the voltage, and never connect blindly.
