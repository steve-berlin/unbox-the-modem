Part 15 — Backups: Why They Matter and How They're Used

The course's golden rule: copy everything before you change anything.

TL;DR

Before you change anything on the device, make a complete copy of its storage. This is the golden rule of the whole course.

A good backup lets you undo a mistake that would otherwise stop the device from starting. Prove the copy is good with a checksum — a short fingerprint that matches only if the copy is exact — and keep more than one copy.

If something breaks, you restore: write the good copy back.

Why back up first?

Everything that makes the device work — the operating system, its settings, its recovery tools — lives in its permanent storage. If a change scrambles the wrong part, the device may no longer start at all, and there may be no button that brings it back.

A backup is a complete copy of that storage, made before you touch anything. It is your undo button. Without it, a single mistake can be permanent. With it, almost any mistake is recoverable.

The golden rule of this course: never change the device until you have a complete, verified backup safely stored somewhere else. Everything hands-on we do later assumes you have one.

The backup → change → restore cycle

Working on a device safely is a loop. You copy first, then change, and if anything goes wrong you put the copy back.

Verifying a backup with a checksum

A copy is only useful if it is exact. A single flipped bit can make a restored copy fail. You prove a copy matches the original with a checksum: a short string of characters worked out from the file's contents, like a fingerprint.

Change even one tiny piece of the file and the checksum comes out completely different. So you take the checksum of the original and of the copy: if the two match, the copy is trustworthy. If they differ, something went wrong and you copy again.

A common tool for this is sha256sum on Linux and Mac, or certutil -hashfile on Windows. Both read a file and print its fingerprint.

Keep more than one copy, and restoring

One copy can be lost — a dead drive, a deleted folder, a corrupted file. Keep at least two copies, ideally in different places (say, on your PC and on a separate USB drive). The effort is tiny next to the cost of losing your only lifeline.

Restoring is the reverse of backing up: you write a good copy back onto the device's storage, returning it to the exact state it was in when you made the backup. Because you verified the copy with a checksum, you can trust that what you write back is correct.

Examples

  • Before changing a single setting, you copy the whole storage to your PC and check its fingerprint — now you can experiment without fear.
  • A change leaves the device unable to start. You write your verified backup back onto it, and it boots again as if nothing happened.
  • You keep one copy on your laptop and a second on a USB stick in a drawer, so a lost laptop doesn't cost you the backup.
  • You copy a file, but its checksum doesn't match the original — a sign the copy is faulty, so you make it again rather than trust it.

Try it yourself (safe)

You'll practise the fingerprint idea on your own PC — no device involved. Pick any small file you don't mind editing (make a copy first if it matters).

On Linux or Mac, open a terminal in the file's folder and run:

sha256sum myfile.txt

On Windows, open Command Prompt and run:

certutil -hashfile myfile.txt SHA256

Note the long string it prints. Now open the file, change one character, save it, and run the same command again. Compare the two fingerprints.

Self-check: what should have happened to the checksum?

It should be completely different — not just slightly changed. Even a one-character edit produces an entirely new fingerprint. That is exactly why a checksum can prove a backup matches the original: if the two fingerprints are identical, the contents are identical; if they differ at all, the copy is not a perfect match and you should make it again.

Practise checksums only on ordinary files on your own computer. Do not run anything against the Nokia device yet — actually copying its storage comes in a later, carefully explained step, and only after you understand the tools.

Recap

  • Always make a complete copy of the device's storage before changing anything — the course's golden rule.
  • A backup is your undo button for mistakes that would otherwise stop the device starting.
  • A checksum is a short fingerprint that proves a copy exactly matches the original.
  • Keep at least two copies, ideally in different places.
  • Restoring means writing a good copy back to return the device to a working state.
